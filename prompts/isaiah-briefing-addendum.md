# The Covenant Rendering — Isaiah Briefing Addendum

## Purpose

Isaiah is the largest prophetic book and the theological summit of the Old Testament. Its 66 chapters span at least two centuries of Israelite history — from the Assyrian crisis (chs. 1-39) through the Babylonian exile (chs. 40-55) to the anticipated return and restoration (chs. 56-66). The book's literary range is unmatched: lawsuit oracles, woe oracles, royal theology, servant songs, cosmic apocalyptic, and some of the most exalted poetry in the Hebrew Bible.

The translator must navigate three challenges simultaneously:

1. **Poetry.** Isaiah is overwhelmingly poetry. Hebrew parallelism, wordplay, assonance, and imagery must be preserved in English without flattening the verse into prose. Line breaks must follow the Hebrew poetic structure.

2. **Theological density.** Isaiah introduces or develops more major theological concepts than any other single book: the Holy One of Israel, the remnant, the Servant, the anointed deliverer, cosmic judgment, new creation, and the suffering that redeems. Every chapter is doing theology.

3. **The Christological question.** Isaiah is the most-quoted Old Testament book in the New Testament. Many passages carry layered meaning — an immediate historical referent and a later messianic reading. The Covenant Rendering translates the Hebrew as the Hebrew stands, without importing later interpretive frameworks into the rendering. When a passage has been read messianically by Jewish or Christian tradition, the notes acknowledge this transparently without privileging any single reading. The Hebrew speaks first.

This addendum supplements (does not replace) the master generation prompt, addendum v1.2, quality correction addendum v1.3, and multi-agent consistency rules. All previous rules remain in full effect.

### 1.2 Prophetic Formulas

These formulas must be rendered consistently throughout Isaiah:

| Hebrew | Rendering | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| כֹּה אָמַר יְהוָה | This is what the LORD says | Locked prophetic messenger formula (per multi-agent consistency rules). Never "Thus says the LORD." |
| נְאֻם יְהוָה | declares the LORD | The divine-speech marker. Rendered consistently. |
| בַּיּוֹם הַהוּא | On that day | The eschatological marker pointing to God's future decisive action. |
| הוֹי | Woe | The funeral-cry turned judgment oracle (chs. 5, 10, 28-33). Render "Woe" consistently. |

## 2. Structural Guide

### 2.1 The Three Major Sections

| Section | Chapters | Setting | Primary themes |
|---|---|---|---|
| First Isaiah | 1-39 | 8th century, Assyrian crisis | Judgment on Judah and the nations, the remnant, Immanuel, the Davidic king, trust vs. political alliances |
| Second Isaiah | 40-55 | Babylonian exile | Comfort, the incomparability of God, the Servant, redemption from exile, Cyrus as God's instrument, new exodus |
| Third Isaiah | 56-66 | Post-exilic restoration | Justice and worship, the faithful community, new heavens and new earth, the tension between promise and reality |

### 2.2 Watch Chapters

These chapters require special theological attention:

| Chapter | Why |
|---|---|
| 1 | The covenant lawsuit — God indicts Israel. Sets the book's entire tone. |
| 2 | The mountain of the LORD — eschatological vision of universal peace. |
| 5 | The vineyard song + six woe oracles. The wordplay mishpat/mispach, tsedaqah/tse'aqah (5:7) is untranslatable but must be noted. |
| 6 | Isaiah's throne-room commissioning. Qadosh qadosh qadosh. The hardening commission. |
| 7 | Immanuel sign to Ahaz. The almah controversy must be handled transparently. |
| 9 | The child born / son given. Four throne names. |
| 11 | The shoot from Jesse's stump. The peaceable kingdom. |
| 14 | The fall of the king of Babylon (helel ben-shachar). |

| 42 | First Servant Song. |

| 49 | Second Servant Song — the Servant's mission to Israel AND the nations. |
| 50 | Third Servant Song — the Servant's suffering and vindication. |

| 65-66 | New heavens and new earth. |

### 2.3 Poetry Rendering

Isaiah is approximately 95% poetry. Every chapter except brief narrative sections (chs. 36-39) must be rendered with:

- **Line breaks** following Hebrew poetic structure (typically 2-3 lines per verse)
- **Parallelism preserved** — synonymous, antithetic, and synthetic parallelism are Isaiah's primary literary devices
- **Imagery intact** — do not explain metaphors in the rendering; let them stand and explain in notes
- **Wordplay noted** — Isaiah is a master of paronomasia. When English cannot reproduce a wordplay, the notes must describe it

## 3. Sensitive Passages

### 3.1 Isaiah 7:14 — The Almah

The word almah (עַלְמָה) means "young woman of marriageable age." The LXX translated it as parthenos ("virgin"), and Matthew 1:23 quotes the LXX. The Hebrew text does not require "virgin" — the word for that is betulah. Render almah as "young woman" in the rendering (following the Hebrew) and provide a thorough translator_note explaining:
- The Hebrew meaning of almah vs. betulah
- The LXX translation choice (parthenos)
- The NT application (Matthew 1:23)
- That both Jewish and Christian traditions have read this passage differently
- The immediate historical context (a sign for Ahaz in the 8th century)

Do NOT resolve the debate. Present all readings transparently.

### 3.2 Isaiah 52:13-53:12 — The Suffering Servant

This is the most important passage in the book and one of the most contested in all of Scripture. Jewish tradition reads it as Israel personified suffering in exile. Christian tradition reads it as messianic prophecy fulfilled in Jesus. Both readings have ancient roots.

Translate from the Hebrew. Let the Hebrew speak. Notes must:
- Explain the Hebrew grammar and vocabulary with precision
- Present both major interpretive traditions without privileging either
- Note where the Hebrew is genuinely ambiguous (e.g., 53:10 — does the Servant "see offspring" or "see light"? Masoretic vs. DSS readings differ)
- Flag this as a prime candidate for the Dead Sea Scrolls stacking feature (1QIsaiah-a)

### 3.3 Isaiah 45:1 — Cyrus as Mashiach

Render it: "This is what the LORD says to His anointed, to Cyrus." Do not soften "anointed" or add qualifiers. Let the notes carry the theological earthquake: a pagan Persian emperor is called God's mashiach. Note that this is the only non-Israelite to receive the title in the Hebrew Bible.

## 5. Generation Plan

Isaiah's 66 chapters should be generated in sections aligned with the book's structure:

| Batch | Chapters | Description | Verses (approx.) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 1-12 | Covenant lawsuit, commissioning, Assyrian crisis, Immanuel, messianic king | 245 |
| 2 | 13-23 | Oracles against the nations | 258 |
| 3 | 24-35 | Isaiah's apocalypse (24-27), woe oracles (28-33), judgment and salvation (34-35) | 296 |
| 4 | 36-39 | Historical narrative (Hezekiah and Sennacherib) — prose, not poetry | 109 |
| 5 | 40-48 | Comfort, incomparability of God, Cyrus, polemic against idols | 229 |
| 6 | 49-55 | Servant Songs, Zion restored, the free invitation | 157 |
| 7 | 56-66 | Post-exilic hope, justice and worship, new heavens and new earth | 198 |

Each batch should be generated, QA'd, and verified before proceeding to the next. Chapters within a batch can be generated in parallel.
