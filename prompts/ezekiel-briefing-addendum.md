# The Covenant Rendering — Ezekiel Briefing Addendum

## Purpose

Ezekiel is the priest-prophet who ministers among the exiles in Babylon. He is both a kohen (priest, son of Buzi, of the Zadokite line) and a navi (prophet) — the only figure in the Hebrew Bible who fully inhabits both offices simultaneously. His priestly training saturates his language: purity categories, Temple architecture, sacrificial procedure, and the kavod (glory) theology that anchors the entire book.

His visions are the most visually intense in the Hebrew Bible. The book divides into three sections: judgment on Israel (chs. 1-24), oracles against the nations (chs. 25-32), and restoration and the new Temple vision (chs. 33-48). The trajectory is theological: the glory departs from the defiled Temple (chs. 8-11), the nations are judged (chs. 25-32), and the glory returns to a purified Temple (ch. 43).

The translator faces three challenges:

1. **Visionary language.** Ezekiel sees things that resist description, and the Hebrew reflects this with unusual vocabulary, stacked similes, and tortured syntax. The throne-chariot vision (ch. 1) piles up comparative particles — "the likeness of," "the appearance of," "something like" — because the prophet is straining language to describe the indescribable. The rendering must preserve this strangeness. Do not smooth Ezekiel's visions into clear prose. If the Hebrew is strange, the English must be strange.

2. **Priestly precision.** The Temple vision (chs. 40-48) is an architectural blueprint rendered in words. Measurements, directional language, and structural terminology must be precise and consistent. The priest in Ezekiel demands exact specifications; the rendering must deliver them.

3. **Graphic allegory.** Chapters 16 and 23 contain the most sexually explicit language in the Hebrew Bible — extended allegories of Jerusalem and Samaria as unfaithful wives and prostitutes. The language is deliberately shocking; it communicates the theological horror of idolatry through visceral imagery. The rendering must translate faithfully from the Hebrew without sanitizing, softening, or euphemizing. The graphic content is the theological argument.

This addendum supplements (does not replace) the master generation prompt, addendum v1.2, quality correction addendum v1.3, and multi-agent consistency rules. All previous rules remain in full effect.

Kavod in Ezekiel is not abstract praise but a near-physical reality that occupies space, moves between locations, and can be seen. It fills the Temple (43:5), departs from the Temple (10:18-19, 11:22-23), and returns to the new Temple (43:1-5). | 1:28, 3:12, 3:23, 8:4, 9:3, 10:4, 10:18-19, 11:22-23, 43:1-5, 44:4 |

Do not flatten to a single rendering — document each choice. | Throughout |
| בֶּן־אָדָם | ben adam | son of man | God's address to Ezekiel throughout the book — approximately 93 occurrences. This is NOT messianic in Ezekiel. ## 2. Prophetic Formulas (Locked)

These formulas must be rendered consistently throughout Ezekiel:

| Hebrew | Rendering | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| כֹּה אָמַר אֲדֹנָי יְהוִה | This is what the Lord GOD says | Locked formula. Ezekiel uses Adonai YHWH, not just YHWH — render as "Lord GOD." Never "Thus says the Lord GOD." |
| נְאֻם אֲדֹנָי יְהוִה | declares the Lord GOD | The divine-speech marker with Ezekiel's compound name. |
| וַיְהִי דְבַר־יְהוָה אֵלַי | The word of the LORD came to me | Standard reception formula. Extremely frequent in Ezekiel. |
| בֶּן־אָדָם | son of man | God addressing Ezekiel — render consistently as "son of man." Note at first occurrence that this means "mortal" and is not the messianic title of Daniel 7 or the Gospels. |
| וַתִּשָּׂא אֹתִי רוּחַ | The Spirit lifted me up | The transport formula — the ruach physically moves Ezekiel between locations in visions. |

## 4. Watch Chapters

| Chapter | Why |
|---|---|
| 1 | Throne-chariot vision (merkavah) — the most elaborate theophany in the Hebrew Bible. The stacked similes and comparative particles ("the likeness of," "the appearance of," "something like") are the prophet straining to describe the indescribable. Preserve the syntactic strangeness. |
| 8-11 | Temple abominations and glory departure. Ezekiel is transported in vision to Jerusalem and witnesses progressively worse idolatry — from image of jealousy (8:3) to women weeping for Tammuz (8:14) to men with their backs to the Temple worshiping the sun (8:16). The kavod departs in stages: from the cherubim to the threshold (9:3, 10:4), from the threshold to the east gate (10:18-19), from the city to the Mount of Olives (11:22-23). |
| 16 | Jerusalem as unfaithful wife — the most sexually explicit allegory in the Hebrew Bible. The foundling girl rescued, adorned, married, and then unfaithful. Render faithfully from the Hebrew. Do not sanitize. |
| 18 | Individual responsibility — "The soul that sins shall die." A theological turning point: the exiles cannot blame their parents' sins for their suffering. Each generation stands or falls on its own faithfulness. |
| 23 | Oholah and Oholibah (Samaria and Jerusalem as prostitutes). Extremely graphic language — more explicit even than ch. 16. The sexual imagery is a theological argument about the horror of political and religious syncretism. Do not soften. |
| 34 | God as shepherd — the LORD will shepherd Israel himself because the human shepherds (kings) have failed. Background text for John 10 and the Good Shepherd tradition. |
| 37 | Valley of dry bones — "Can these bones live?" Both a national restoration prophecy (the exiles will return) and, in Christian reading, a resurrection text. Present both readings. The two sticks (37:15-28) — Judah and Israel reunited under one king. |
| 40-48 | New Temple vision — detailed architectural measurements and liturgical ordinances. The priest in Ezekiel emerges fully. This is not metaphor; it is a blueprint (whether literal or visionary is debated). Measurements must be precise and consistent. |
| 43 | The glory of the LORD returns to the new Temple from the east — reversing the departure of chs. 10-11. The structural climax of the entire book. |
| 47 | The river flowing from the Temple — water that deepens as it flows, bringing life to the Dead Sea. Connects to Revelation 22:1-2 (river of life from the throne). Note the connection. |

## 5. Sensitive Passages

### 5.1 Chapters 16 and 23 — The Sexual Allegories

Translate from the Hebrew faithfully. These chapters contain the most sexually explicit language in the Hebrew Bible, and that explicitness is theologically intentional — it communicates the horror of idolatry and political syncretism through visceral, degrading imagery. The prophet wants his audience to be shocked.

Do NOT sanitize, euphemize, or soften. Where the Hebrew is graphic, the English must be graphic. Preamble notes should:
- Acknowledge that these chapters have always been controversial within Judaism — the Mishnah (Megillah 4:10) restricts the public reading of ch. 16 as a haftarah
- Explain the allegorical structure: the sexual imagery represents covenant unfaithfulness, not literal sexual activity
- Note that the violence described against the unfaithful wife reflects ancient Near Eastern treaty-curse language, not a prescription for domestic violence
- Flag the pastoral sensitivity without suppressing the text

### 5.2 Chapter 37 — The Valley of Dry Bones

Both a national restoration prophecy and (in Christian reading) a resurrection text. The immediate context is clear: the bones are "the whole house of Israel" (37:11), and the vision promises national restoration from exile. But the imagery of bodily resurrection (bones, sinews, flesh, breath/spirit) became foundational for later Jewish and Christian resurrection theology. Notes must:
- Present the primary meaning: national restoration of exiled Israel
- Present the secondary reading: bodily resurrection (developed in Daniel 12:2, 2 Maccabees 7, and the NT)
- Note the ambiguity of ruach in 37:9-10 — "breath," "wind," and "spirit" all present simultaneously

### 5.3 Chapter 44:9 — Exclusion of Uncircumcised Foreigners

Ezekiel 44:9 excludes uncircumcised foreigners from the new Temple. This stands in tension with Isaiah 56:3-7, which explicitly welcomes foreigners who keep the Sabbath and hold fast to the covenant. Notes must:
- Present both texts without harmonizing
- Note that Ezekiel's priestly perspective emphasizes holiness boundaries while Isaiah's prophetic perspective emphasizes universal inclusion
- Acknowledge the theological tension honestly — the Hebrew Bible does not always speak with one voice on every question

### 5.4 Chapters 38-39 — Gog and Magog

The Gog oracle has generated enormous speculative interpretation across centuries. Notes should:
- Present the text as Ezekiel gives it — an eschatological enemy from the north
- Note that Gog and Magog reappear in Revelation 20:8
- Do NOT identify Gog with any modern nation or geopolitical entity — the text is eschatological, not predictive journalism
