# The Covenant Rendering — Jeremiah Briefing Addendum

## Purpose

Jeremiah is the most personal of the major prophets — the "weeping prophet" whose inner life, doubts, anger, and anguish are woven into his oracles. His ministry spans the final decades of Judah (c. 627–586 BCE), from Josiah's reforms through the Babylonian destruction of Jerusalem. The book is not chronological — oracles, narratives, confessions, and sermons are arranged thematically and redactionally, not by date.

The translator faces three challenges:

1. **Mixed genres.** Jeremiah alternates between poetry (oracles, confessions, laments) and prose (biographical narratives, sermons, letters). The shift must be clear in the rendering — poetry with line breaks, prose as paragraphs.

2. **Emotional intensity.** Jeremiah's confessions (11:18-12:6, 15:10-21, 17:14-18, 18:18-23, 20:7-18) are the most raw prayers in the Hebrew Bible. The prophet accuses God of deception (20:7), curses the day of his birth (20:14-18), and demands vengeance on his enemies. The rendering must preserve this emotional violence without softening.

3. **The New Covenant.** Jeremiah 31:31-34 introduces the berit chadashah ("new covenant") — the only place in the Hebrew Bible where this exact phrase appears. It is the most consequential theological concept in the book, foundational to both Jewish and Christian theology. Handle with maximum transparency.

## 2. Prophetic Formulas (Locked)

- **"This is what the LORD says"** — ko amar YHWH (never "Thus says")
- **"declares the LORD"** — ne'um YHWH
- **"the word of the LORD came to..."** — vayehi devar-YHWH el...
- **"the Holy One of Israel"** — always full (rare in Jeremiah but present)

## 5. Sensitive Passages

### 31:31-34 — The New Covenant
Translate from the Hebrew. The phrase berit chadashah appears only here in the entire Hebrew Bible. Notes must cover:
- The Hebrew meaning: a genuinely new covenant, not a renewal of the old
- The Sinai covenant that "they broke" (v. 32) — God as the wronged party
- "I will put my torah in their minds and write it on their hearts" (v. 33) — torah internalized
- "They will all know me" (v. 34) — universal direct knowledge of God
- The NT citation: Hebrews 8:8-12 quotes this passage as the theological basis for the new covenant in Christ
- Jewish reading: the new covenant is with Israel, written on Jewish hearts, fulfilled in Torah observance renewed
- Present both without privileging either

### 20:7-18 — Jeremiah's Accusation Against God
The verb pittitani (v. 7) means "you deceived me" or "you enticed me" — the prophet accuses God of seduction or deception. Do NOT soften this. The Hebrew is confrontational and the rendering must be equally confrontational. Note the range of pittah (to entice, deceive, seduce, persuade) and let the reader feel the prophet's rage.
