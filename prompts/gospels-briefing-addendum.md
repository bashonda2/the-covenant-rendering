# The Covenant Rendering — Gospels Briefing Addendum

## Purpose

The four Gospels — Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John — form the narrative foundation of the New Testament. They present a single story through four distinct theological lenses: Matthew the Jewish teacher, Mark the urgent narrator, Luke the historian-theologian, and John the mystic theologian. Together they contain approximately 3,780 verses covering Jesus' life, teaching, death, and resurrection.

The translator must navigate four challenges simultaneously:

1. **Synoptic interdependence.** Matthew, Mark, and Luke share large blocks of material, sometimes word-for-word. When the same Greek phrase appears in parallel passages, the rendering must be consistent unless a genuine textual variant or theological distinction justifies divergence. The reader who compares parallel passages must find coherence, not arbitrary variation.

2. **Hebrew substrate.** Jesus spoke Aramaic. The Gospels are written in Greek but frequently preserve Semitic syntax, idioms, and theological vocabulary rooted in the Hebrew Bible. The translator must hear the Hebrew/Aramaic behind the Greek — especially in quotations from the OT, parables, and liturgical formulas.

3. **Genre diversity.** The Gospels contain narrative prose, poetic discourse (Beatitudes, Magnificat, Benedictus), parables, apocalyptic discourse (Olivet), dialogue, prayer, and legal argumentation. Each genre requires its own rendering approach.

4. **Christological weight.** Every title, claim, and action attributed to Jesus carries theological freight that centuries of tradition have loaded further. The Covenant Rendering translates the Greek as the Greek stands, noting interpretive traditions without privileging any single reading over the text itself.

This addendum supplements (does not replace) the master generation prompt, addendum v1.2, quality correction addendum v1.3, and multi-agent consistency rules. All previous rules remain in full effect.

## 1. Gospels Vocabulary

### 1.1 Core Terms — Shared Across All Four Gospels

| Greek | Transliteration | Rendering | Theology | Key passages |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| βασιλεία τοῦ θεοῦ / βασιλεία τῶν οὐρανῶν | basileia tou theou / basileia tōn ouranōn | the kingdom of God / the kingdom of heaven | Matthew consistently uses "kingdom of heaven" (Jewish circumlocution avoiding God's name); Mark, Luke, and John use "kingdom of God." Preserve Matthew's distinctive usage — do NOT harmonize to "kingdom of God" throughout. Both phrases refer to the same reality; the note should explain the Matthean convention. | Matt 3:2, 4:17, 5:3, 13:11; Mark 1:15, 4:11; Luke 4:43, 17:20-21; John 3:3, 3:5 |
| παραβολή | parabolē | parable | A comparison narrative or saying. Render "parable" consistently. Note that Mark 4:11-12 uses the term in connection with Isaiah 6:9-10 (hardening) — cross-reference TCR's Isaiah rendering. | Matt 13:3, 13:34; Mark 4:2, 4:33; Luke 8:4, 15:3 |
| μαθητής | mathētēs | disciple | One who learns by following. Render "disciple" consistently, never "student" or "follower" (except where the Greek uses akolouthōn). | Throughout all four Gospels |
| ὁ υἱὸς τοῦ ἀνθρώπου | ho huios tou anthrōpou | the Son of Man | **LOCKED RENDERING.** Jesus' preferred self-designation. The phrase echoes Daniel 7:13-14 (bar enash) — the human figure who receives universal dominion from the Ancient of Days. Cross-reference TCR's Daniel rendering. The notes must explain: (a) the Daniel 7 background, (b) the Aramaic bar enash, (c) the three categories of Son of Man sayings (earthly authority, suffering, eschatological glory). Never render as "the Human One" or "the Son of Humanity." | Matt 8:20, 16:13, 24:30, 26:64; Mark 2:10, 8:31, 13:26, 14:62; Luke 9:58, 17:22, 22:69; John 1:51, 3:13, 6:53, 13:31 |
| χριστός | christos | Christ / the Anointed One | The Greek equivalent of Hebrew mashiach. In narrative context where it functions as a title, render "the Christ" or "the Anointed One." Where it has become a proper name (as in some Pauline usage), "Christ" without the article. In the Gospels, it almost always functions as a title. Cross-reference TCR's OT mashiach rendering. | Matt 1:1, 16:16, 26:63; Mark 8:29, 14:61; Luke 2:11, 9:20; John 1:41, 4:25, 20:31 |
| κύριος | kyrios | Lord / lord / master | Context-dependent: divine title ("Lord"), social address ("lord/master"), or the LXX rendering of YHWH in OT quotations ("the LORD"). Document each choice. When an OT quotation using kyrios maps to YHWH in the Hebrew, the note must flag this. | Throughout |
| πνεῦμα ἅγιον | pneuma hagion | the Holy Spirit | Render consistently. Note that the Hebrew ruach haqqodesh lies behind the Greek. | Matt 1:18, 3:11; Mark 1:8; Luke 1:35, 4:1; John 14:26, 20:22 |
| εὐαγγέλιον | euangelion | good news / the gospel | Render "good news" in narrative and discourse contexts. "The gospel" is acceptable in formulaic or summary statements. Never "glad tidings." | Matt 4:23, 24:14; Mark 1:1, 1:15; Luke 4:18 (quoting Isa 61); John does not use the noun |
| ἁμαρτία | hamartia | sin | Missing the mark. Render "sin" consistently. | Matt 1:21, 9:2; Mark 2:5; Luke 5:20, 24:47; John 1:29, 8:34 |
| μετάνοια / μετανοέω | metanoia / metanoeō | repentance / repent | A turning of the whole person — mind, will, direction. Render "repent/repentance" consistently. Note the Hebrew teshuvah behind it. | Matt 3:2, 4:17; Mark 1:4, 1:15, 6:12; Luke 3:3, 13:3, 15:7 |
| πίστις / πιστεύω | pistis / pisteuō | faith / believe | The noun-verb pair is central: pistis = trust/faithfulness/faith; pisteuō = to trust/believe. Render "faith" and "believe" as defaults; note when "faithfulness" or "trust" better captures the sense. John overwhelmingly uses the verb, almost never the noun. | Matt 8:10, 9:2, 17:20; Mark 5:34, 11:22; Luke 7:50, 18:8; John 3:16, 6:29, 11:25-26, 20:31 |

### 1.2 Johannine Distinctives

John's vocabulary diverges significantly from the Synoptics. These terms require special attention:

| Greek | Transliteration | Rendering | Theology | Key passages |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| λόγος | logos | the Word | **LOCKED RENDERING** in John 1:1-14. The Logos is John's unique christological title, resonating with both Hebrew dabar (God's creative/prophetic speech) and Greek philosophical tradition (the rational principle of the cosmos). The notes must present both backgrounds without collapsing one into the other. After the Prologue, John rarely uses logos as a christological title — context determines rendering elsewhere. | John 1:1, 1:14; 1 John 1:1; Rev 19:13 |
| ἀλήθεια | alētheia | truth | In John, truth is not merely factual accuracy but divine reality revealed. The phrase "Spirit of truth" (pneuma tēs alētheias) recurs in the Farewell Discourse. Render "truth" consistently. | John 1:14, 1:17, 4:23-24, 8:32, 14:6, 14:17, 16:13, 17:17, 18:37-38 |
| ζωή | zōē | life | Specifically eternal/divine life, not biological existence (for which Greek uses bios). John uses zōē 36 times. Render "life" consistently; qualify as "eternal life" (zōē aiōnios) where the Greek does. | John 1:4, 3:15-16, 5:24, 6:35, 10:10, 11:25, 14:6, 17:3, 20:31 |
| φῶς | phōs | light | The light/darkness dualism structures John's entire theology. Light = revelation, divine presence, truth. Darkness = ignorance, rejection, evil. Render "light" and "darkness" consistently. | John 1:4-5, 1:9, 3:19-21, 8:12, 9:5, 12:35-36, 12:46 |
| μένω | menō | remain / abide | John's characteristic relational verb: to remain in, dwell in, abide in. Render "remain" as the default (modern, accessible). "Abide" is acceptable in the vine discourse (John 15) if the register calls for it, but "remain" is preferred for consistency. | John 1:38-39, 6:56, 8:31, 14:10, 15:4-10, 1 John 2:6, 2:24, 4:13 |
| ἐγώ εἰμι | egō eimi | I am | **LOCKED RENDERING.** Jesus' seven "I am" statements with predicates (bread of life, light of the world, the door, the good shepherd, the resurrection and the life, the way/truth/life, the true vine) plus the absolute "I am" (8:58, 13:19, 18:5-6) which echoes the divine name (Exodus 3:14 LXX). The notes must flag the Exodus 3 connection for the absolute uses. Never paraphrase or soften. | John 6:35, 8:12, 8:58, 10:7, 10:11, 11:25, 14:6, 15:1, 18:5 |
| σημεῖον | sēmeion | sign | John's preferred term for Jesus' miraculous works (never "miracle" — John avoids thauma/dynamis). A sign points beyond itself to Jesus' identity. Render "sign" consistently. | John 2:11, 2:18, 4:54, 6:14, 6:30, 12:18, 20:30 |
| δόξα / δοξάζω | doxa / doxazō | glory / glorify | In John, Jesus' glorification encompasses both his death and his resurrection — the cross is paradoxically the throne. Render "glory/glorify" consistently. Note the Hebrew kavod behind doxa. | John 1:14, 2:11, 7:39, 11:4, 12:23, 12:28, 13:31-32, 17:1, 17:5, 17:22 |

### 1.3 Liturgical Formulas and Locked Phrasings

These phrasings are **LOCKED** and must be rendered identically every time they appear:

| Passage | Locked Rendering | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| The Beatitudes (Matt 5:3-12) | "Blessed are..." for each makarios statement | Never "Happy are" or "Fortunate are." Makarios carries covenantal weight: the divine approval that transcends circumstance. |
| The Lord's Prayer (Matt 6:9-13 / Luke 11:2-4) | "Our Father in heaven, hallowed be Your name. Your kingdom come, Your will be done, on earth as it is in heaven. Give us this day our daily bread, and forgive us our debts, as we also have forgiven our debtors. And lead us not into temptation, but deliver us from evil." | Matthew's longer form is the base rendering. Luke's shorter form is rendered as the Greek gives it. The doxology ("For Yours is the kingdom...") is a later addition — include it in brackets with a textual note. "Debts" (opheilēmata) in Matthew, "sins" (hamartias) in Luke — render each as the Greek gives it. |
| The Great Commission (Matt 28:18-20) | "All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to Me. Go therefore and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, teaching them to observe all that I have commanded you. And behold, I am with you always, to the end of the age." | Render precisely. "Make disciples" (mathēteusate) is the main imperative. "Baptizing" and "teaching" are participial, describing how discipleship happens. |
| John 1:1 | "In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God." | **LOCKED.** The three clauses build: existence, relationship, identity. Do not restructure. The note must address the absence of the article before theos in the third clause and the range of translations debated. |
| John 1:14 | "And the Word became flesh and dwelt among us, and we have seen His glory, glory as of the only Son from the Father, full of grace and truth." | "Dwelt" (eskēnōsen) literally "tabernacled" — the note must connect this to the OT mishkan/shekinah theology. Cross-reference TCR's tabernacle vocabulary. |
| John 3:16 | "For God so loved the world, that He gave His only Son, that whoever believes in Him should not perish but have eternal life." | Render precisely. "So loved" (houtōs ēgapēsen) means "loved in this way/to this degree" — not merely "loved very much." Note this. |

## 2. Structural Guide

### 2.1 Synoptic Relationships

The translator must be aware of source relationships without allowing them to override the text:

| Relationship | Description | Translation implication |
|---|---|---|
| Markan priority | Mark was likely written first; Matthew and Luke both used Mark | When parallel passages share identical Greek, render them identically. When they diverge, respect the divergence — each evangelist made deliberate editorial choices. |
| Q material | Material shared by Matthew and Luke but absent from Mark (e.g., Beatitudes, Lord's Prayer, temptation narrative) | Matthew and Luke often have different versions of Q sayings. Do NOT harmonize — render each as the Greek gives it. |
| M material | Material unique to Matthew (e.g., Magi, Peter walking on water, parables of wheat/tares, sheep/goats) | Render with attention to Matthew's Jewish-Christian audience: fulfillment quotations, Torah interpretation, ecclesial concerns. |
| L material | Material unique to Luke (e.g., birth narrative details, Good Samaritan, Prodigal Son, Emmaus road) | Render with attention to Luke's literary elegance, concern for outsiders, and Septuagintal style. |
| John's independence | John shares minimal material with the Synoptics; his sources, structure, and theology are largely independent | John requires its own vocabulary register (see 1.2 above). Do not force Synoptic parallels onto John. |

### 2.2 Fulfillment Quotations (Matthew)

Matthew contains approximately 60 OT quotations, including a distinctive formula: "This was to fulfill what was spoken by the prophet..." (hina/hopōs plērōthē to rhēthen). These are critical for TCR because they create direct cross-references to the OT volumes already rendered.

**Rules:**

1. Render the OT quotation as Matthew's Greek gives it (which often follows the LXX, not the MT).
2. In the translator_note, provide TCR's Hebrew-based rendering from the relevant OT book for comparison.
3. Note divergences between the LXX-based quotation and the Hebrew original.
4. Never silently harmonize Matthew's quotation with the OT rendering — the differences are theologically significant.

Key fulfillment quotations:

| Passage | OT Source | Issue |
|---|---|---|
| Matt 1:23 | Isa 7:14 | parthenos (virgin) vs. almah (young woman) — cross-reference TCR's Isaiah rendering |
| Matt 2:6 | Micah 5:2 | Composite quotation, partly adapted |
| Matt 2:15 | Hos 11:1 | "Out of Egypt I called my son" — originally about Israel, applied to Jesus |
| Matt 2:18 | Jer 31:15 | Rachel weeping — cross-reference TCR's Jeremiah rendering |
| Matt 4:15-16 | Isa 9:1-2 | Galilee of the nations |
| Matt 8:17 | Isa 53:4 | "He took our illnesses" — note Matthew's physical reading of the Servant Song |
| Matt 12:18-21 | Isa 42:1-4 | First Servant Song — longest fulfillment quotation in Matthew |
| Matt 21:5 | Zech 9:9 | The king riding on a donkey |
| Matt 27:9-10 | Zech 11:12-13 / Jer 32:6-9 | Attributed to Jeremiah — note the attribution question |

### 2.3 Watch Chapters

| Chapter(s) | Why |
|---|---|
| Matt 1-2 | Genealogy, birth narrative, fulfillment quotations. Genealogy structure (3x14) is numerically deliberate — note the Davidic emphasis. |
| Matt 5-7 | Sermon on the Mount. The most sustained block of Jesus' ethical teaching. Six antitheses ("You have heard... but I say...") reinterpret Torah. Render with care for the Hebraic rhetorical structure. |
| Matt 24-25 | Olivet Discourse. Apocalyptic material paralleling Mark 13 and Luke 21. The "abomination of desolation" (bdelygma tēs erēmōseōs) must cross-reference Daniel 9:27, 11:31, 12:11 in TCR. |
| Matt 26-28 | Passion and resurrection. Last Supper words, Gethsemane, trial, crucifixion, Great Commission. |
| Mark 1 | The beginning of the Gospel. Mark's urgent opening — euthys ("immediately") sets the pace. |
| Mark 13 | The Little Apocalypse. Mark's version of the Olivet Discourse. |
| Mark 15-16 | Passion, death, and the ending problem (see 3.2 below). |
| Luke 1-2 | Birth narratives with the Magnificat (1:46-55), Benedictus (1:68-79), Gloria (2:14), and Nunc Dimittis (2:29-32). These are Hebrew-style poems in Greek — render as poetry with line breaks. |
| Luke 15 | The three "lost" parables: sheep, coin, son. The Prodigal Son is the longest parable in the Gospels. |
| Luke 24 | Emmaus road — "He opened the Scriptures to them." The hermeneutical key to the entire Gospel. |
| John 1 | The Prologue. The theological gateway to the entire Gospel. Every word is load-bearing. |
| John 6 | Bread of Life discourse. Eucharistic language: "my flesh... my blood." |
| John 13-17 | The Farewell Discourse. The longest continuous block of Jesus' speech in any Gospel. Includes the foot washing (13), the way/truth/life (14), the vine (15), the Paraclete promises (14-16), and the High Priestly Prayer (17). |
| John 18-21 | Passion, death, resurrection, and the appendix (ch 21). |

## 3. Sensitive Passages

### 3.1 The Pericope Adulterae (John 7:53-8:11)

This passage — the woman caught in adultery — is almost certainly not original to John's Gospel. The earliest and best manuscripts lack it; its style and vocabulary differ from John; some manuscripts place it after Luke 21:38 or elsewhere. However, it has been part of the canonical text since at least the 4th century and is beloved by readers.

**Handling:**

- Include the passage in the text but mark it with double brackets: ⟦John 7:53-8:11⟧
- The preamble must explain: this passage is absent from the earliest manuscripts, its textual history is complex, and scholars widely regard it as a later addition — but one that may preserve an authentic tradition about Jesus.
- Render the Greek as it stands in the Majority Text / Byzantine tradition.
- Do NOT omit it. Do NOT present it without the textual note.

### 3.2 The Longer Ending of Mark (Mark 16:9-20)

Mark's Gospel almost certainly ends at 16:8 — "and they said nothing to anyone, for they were afraid." The Longer Ending (16:9-20) is absent from the earliest manuscripts and differs in style and vocabulary from the rest of Mark. A Shorter Ending also exists in some manuscripts.

**Handling:**

- Render Mark 16:1-8 as the main text.
- Include 16:9-20 in brackets with a textual note: "The following verses are not found in the earliest manuscripts. Two alternative endings circulated in the early church."
- Also note the Shorter Ending in the translator_notes.
- Do NOT treat the snake-handling/poison-drinking passage (16:17-18) as normative teaching.

### 3.3 The Comma Johanneum (1 John 5:7-8)

While technically in an epistle, this textual issue is Johannine and should be flagged here: the Trinitarian formula ("the Father, the Word, and the Holy Spirit, and these three are one") is a late addition not found in any Greek manuscript before the 16th century. Do NOT include it in the rendering. Note its history in the translator_notes.

### 3.4 Jesus' Last Words on the Cross

Each Gospel records different final words. Do NOT harmonize:

| Gospel | Words | OT Background |
|---|---|---|
| Matt 27:46 / Mark 15:34 | "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" (Eli/Eloi, lama sabachthani) | Psalm 22:1 — render the Aramaic, translate it, and cross-reference TCR's Psalm 22. |
| Luke 23:46 | "Father, into your hands I commit my spirit" | Psalm 31:5 — cross-reference TCR's Psalm 31. |
| John 19:30 | "It is finished" (tetelestai) | The perfect tense: it stands completed. Note the possible connection to the sacrificial system. |

## 4. Term Rendering Decisions for the Gospels

These supplement the Default Term Register in the multi-agent consistency rules:

| Term | Default | Gospels-specific guidance |

| basileia tōn ouranōn | the kingdom of heaven | Matthew only. Do not change to "kingdom of God." |
| basileia tou theou | the kingdom of God | Mark, Luke, John. Do not change to "kingdom of heaven." |
| ho huios tou anthrōpou | the Son of Man | LOCKED. Never "the Human One." |

| skandalon / skandalizō | stumbling block / cause to stumble | Never "offend" in the modern sense. |
| gehenna | Gehenna | Transliterate, do not translate as "hell." Note the Valley of Hinnom background. |

| aiōnios | eternal / of the age | Default "eternal." Note that the word relates to aiōn (age) and some scholars read it as "of the coming age" rather than "everlasting." Present both; do not resolve. |
| makarios | blessed | LOCKED in the Beatitudes. Never "happy." |
| eskēnōsen (John 1:14) | dwelt | Note: literally "tabernacled." Cross-reference mishkan. |

## 5. Generation Plan

The four Gospels should be generated in the following order and batches:

| Batch | Book | Chapters | Description | Verses (approx.) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Mark | 1-8 | Galilean ministry, miracles, parables, feeding of 5000 | 355 |
| 2 | Mark | 9-16 | Transfiguration, journey to Jerusalem, passion, ending | 323 |
| 3 | Matthew | 1-7 | Birth, Sermon on the Mount | 195 |
| 4 | Matthew | 8-15 | Miracles, parables discourse, controversies | 300 |
| 5 | Matthew | 16-22 | Peter's confession, transfiguration, Jerusalem entry | 267 |
| 6 | Matthew | 23-28 | Woes, Olivet Discourse, passion, resurrection | 244 |
| 7 | Luke | 1-6 | Birth narratives, Nazareth sermon, Sermon on the Plain | 270 |
| 8 | Luke | 7-12 | Ministry, parables, sending of the 72 | 302 |
| 9 | Luke | 13-19 | Journey to Jerusalem, "lost" parables, Zacchaeus | 278 |
| 10 | Luke | 20-24 | Temple controversies, Olivet, passion, resurrection, Emmaus | 238 |
| 11 | John | 1-6 | Prologue, signs, bread of life | 303 |
| 12 | John | 7-12 | Feast discourses, Lazarus, triumphal entry | 262 |
| 13 | John | 13-17 | Farewell Discourse, High Priestly Prayer | 196 |
| 14 | John | 18-21 | Passion, resurrection, appendix | 156 |

**Rationale for order:** Mark first (likely earliest, shortest, provides the narrative template). Matthew second (expands Mark with Jewish-Christian theology). Luke third (expands Mark with Gentile-inclusive theology). John last (independent tradition, requires familiarity with Synoptic renderings for cross-reference).

Each batch should be generated, QA'd, and verified before proceeding to the next. Within each batch, chapters can be generated in parallel.
