# The Covenant Rendering — Romans Briefing Addendum

## Purpose

Romans is Paul's most systematic theological letter. Written to a church he had not yet visited (likely c. 57 CE from Corinth), it presents his most sustained argument about the righteousness of God, the universality of sin, justification by faith, the role of the Law, the fate of Israel, and the shape of the redeemed life. Its 16 chapters and 433 verses have generated more theological commentary than perhaps any other biblical book.

The translator must navigate three challenges simultaneously:

1. **Argumentative density.** Romans is not narrative or poetry — it is sustained theological argumentation in Koine Greek, using rhetorical conventions (diatribe, prosopopoeia, catena) that must be rendered clearly without flattening Paul's style. Paul's sentences can run for multiple verses; the rendering must preserve the logical flow without becoming unreadable.

2. **OT saturation.** Romans contains approximately 60 direct OT quotations and dozens more allusions. Many are strung together in catenas (chains of quotations). Each quotation must be cross-referenced to TCR's OT rendering, and divergences between Paul's Greek (usually LXX-based) and the Hebrew original must be noted.

3. **Theological vocabulary.** Romans deploys a technical vocabulary — righteousness, justification, faith, law, grace, sin, flesh, spirit — that has been the battlefield of centuries of theological debate (Catholic vs. Protestant, Old Perspective vs. New Perspective on Paul). The Covenant Rendering translates the Greek as the Greek stands and presents interpretive options in the notes without resolving debates that the text itself leaves open.

This addendum supplements (does not replace) the master generation prompt, addendum v1.2, quality correction addendum v1.3, and multi-agent consistency rules. All previous rules remain in full effect.

## 1. Romans Vocabulary

### 1.1 Core Theological Terms

| Greek | Transliteration | Rendering | Theology | Key passages |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| δικαιοσύνη θεοῦ | dikaiosynē theou | the righteousness of God | The thesis term of the letter. The genitive "of God" is ambiguous: it can mean God's own righteousness (attribute), the righteousness that comes from God (gift), or God's covenant faithfulness (relational). Do NOT resolve this — render "the righteousness of God" and let the notes present all three major readings. Cross-reference TCR's rendering of tsedaqah in Isaiah, Psalms, and the prophets. | 1:17, 3:5, 3:21-22, 3:25-26, 10:3 |
| δικαιόω | dikaioō | justify / declare righteous | The verb of justification. Render "justify" as default. The notes must explain the range: forensic declaration (Protestant reading), transformative making-righteous (Catholic reading), covenant vindication (New Perspective reading). All three have textual warrant; none should be privileged. | 2:13, 3:4, 3:20, 3:24, 3:26, 3:28, 3:30, 4:2, 4:5, 5:1, 5:9, 6:7, 8:30, 8:33 |
| πίστις Χριστοῦ | pistis Christou | faith in Christ / the faithfulness of Christ | One of the most debated phrases in Pauline studies. The genitive can be objective ("faith directed toward Christ") or subjective ("Christ's own faithfulness"). Render "faith in Christ" in the text (the majority reading) but note the subjective genitive option prominently. This applies to all pistis + genitive constructions. | 3:22, 3:26; cf. Gal 2:16, 2:20, 3:22; Phil 3:9 |
| πίστις | pistis | faith | The human response to God's act. Render "faith" as default. Note that pistis can also mean "faithfulness" or "trust" — context determines which shade predominates. In Romans 4 (Abraham), pistis is specifically trust in God's promise. | 1:5, 1:8, 1:12, 1:17, 3:22, 3:25, 3:27-28, 3:30-31, 4:5, 4:9, 4:11-14, 4:16, 4:19-20, 5:1-2, 9:30, 9:32, 10:6, 10:8, 10:17, 12:3, 12:6, 14:1, 14:22-23, 16:26 |
| νόμος | nomos | the Law / law | When referring to the Mosaic Torah, render "the Law" (capitalized, with article). Cross-reference TCR's torah rendering. | 2:12-27, 3:19-21, 3:27-31, 4:13-16, 5:13, 5:20, 6:14-15, 7:1-25, 8:2-4, 9:31, 10:4-5, 13:8-10 |
| χάρις | charis | grace | Unmerited divine favor. Render "grace" consistently. In Romans, charis stands in antithesis to nomos and erga (works) as the ground of justification. | 1:5, 1:7, 3:24, 4:4, 4:16, 5:2, 5:15-21, 6:1, 6:14-15, 11:5-6, 12:3, 12:6, 15:15, 16:20 |
| ἁμαρτία | hamartia | sin | In Romans, hamartia is not just individual acts but a cosmic power that enslaves (5:12-21, 6:1-23, 7:7-25). Paul personifies sin as a tyrant. Render "sin" consistently but note when it functions as a power/agent rather than an act. | 1:18-32 (described), 3:9, 3:20, 3:23, 5:12-21, 6:1-23, 7:7-25, 8:1-3 |
| σάρξ | sarx | flesh | NOT simply the physical body. In Paul, sarx denotes the whole human person oriented away from God — the sphere of weakness, mortality, and rebellion. Render "flesh" consistently and explain in notes. Never "sinful nature" (interpretive paraphrase). | 1:3, 4:1, 7:5, 7:14, 7:18, 7:25, 8:1-13, 9:3, 9:5, 13:14 |
| πνεῦμα | pneuma | Spirit / spirit | When referring to God's Spirit, capitalize: "the Spirit." When referring to the human spirit or a general disposition, lowercase. Romans 8 is the great Spirit chapter — pneuma appears 21 times. | 1:4, 2:29, 5:5, 7:6, 8:1-27, 8:9-16, 8:23, 8:26-27, 9:1, 12:11, 14:17, 15:13, 15:16, 15:19, 15:30 |
| ἔργα νόμου | erga nomou | works of the Law | The phrase that anchors the justification debate. Render "works of the Law" consistently. The notes must explain: does this mean (a) human efforts to earn favor by obeying Torah, (b) specifically the identity markers of Torah observance (circumcision, food laws, Sabbath), or (c) the deeds the Law demands? All three readings have scholarly support. | 3:20, 3:28; cf. Gal 2:16, 3:2, 3:5, 3:10 |
| ἀπολύτρωσις | apolytrōsis | redemption | The act of buying back / liberating — from the slave market metaphor. Cross-reference TCR's go'el/padah vocabulary from the OT. | 3:24, 8:23 |
| ἱλαστήριον | hilastērion | mercy seat / propitiation / place of atonement | In 3:25, God set forth Christ as a hilastērion. The word can mean "propitiation" (turning away wrath), "expiation" (removing sin), or "mercy seat" (the kapporet in the tabernacle, Lev 16). Render "mercy seat" in the text — it is the most literal and connects directly to TCR's Leviticus rendering. Note all three options. | 3:25 |
| κατάκριμα / κατακρίνω | katakrima / katakrinō | condemnation / condemn | The verdict that hamartia brings. Romans 8:1 ("no condemnation") reverses what 5:16-18 established. Render consistently. | 5:16, 5:18, 8:1, 8:3, 8:34 |
| ἐλπίς | elpis | hope | Not wishful thinking but confident expectation grounded in God's character. Render "hope" consistently. | 4:18, 5:2, 5:4-5, 8:20, 8:24-25, 12:12, 15:4, 15:13 |

### 1.2 Rhetorical Formulas

Paul uses distinctive rhetorical devices throughout Romans. These must be rendered consistently:

| Greek Formula | Rendering | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| τί οὖν ἐροῦμεν; | What then shall we say? | The diatribe opening. Appears 6 times (3:5, 4:1, 6:1, 7:7, 8:31, 9:14, 9:30). Render identically each time. |
| μὴ γένοιτο | By no means! | Paul's emphatic rejection of a false inference. Appears 10 times in Romans. Never "God forbid" (KJV) — there is no mention of God. Never "Absolutely not!" (too casual). "By no means!" captures the force. LOCKED. |
| οὐ θέλω ὑμᾶς ἀγνοεῖν | I do not want you to be ignorant / I want you to know | Paul's disclosure formula. Render consistently. |
| ἀδελφοί | brothers / brothers and sisters | Paul addresses the Roman church. Render "brothers and sisters" since adelphoi is used generically for the mixed congregation. Note this in the first occurrence. |

## 2. Structural Guide

### 2.1 The Argument of Romans

| Section | Chapters | Theme | Key terms |
|---|---|---|---|
| Introduction | 1:1-17 | The gospel Paul preaches; thesis statement (1:16-17) | euangelion, dikaiosynē theou, pistis |
| The Problem | 1:18-3:20 | The universal reality of sin — Gentile (1:18-32), Jewish (2:1-3:8), all humanity (3:9-20) | orgē (wrath), hamartia, nomos |
| The Solution | 3:21-4:25 | Justification by faith, apart from the Law; Abraham as the exemplar | dikaiosynē, pistis, charis, hilastērion |
| The Result | 5:1-8:39 | Peace, hope, freedom from sin/law/death, life in the Spirit | eirēnē, elpis, sarx, pneuma, agapē |
| The Problem of Israel | 9:1-11:36 | Israel's election, stumbling, and ultimate salvation | eklogē, sklērynō (harden), mystery, "all Israel" |
| The Transformed Life | 12:1-15:13 | Ethical instruction — the renewed mind, the body of Christ, love, governing authorities, the weak and the strong | logikē latreia, agapē, syneidēsis |
| Closing | 15:14-16:27 | Travel plans, greetings, doxology | — |

### 2.2 Watch Passages

| Passage | Why |
|---|---|
| 1:16-17 | **The thesis of the entire letter.** "The righteousness of God is revealed from faith to faith" (ek pisteōs eis pistin). Every word is debated. Render precisely and let the notes carry the weight. |
| 1:18-32 | The "wrath" passage describing Gentile idolatry and its consequences. Verse 26-27 (same-sex relations) is one of the most contested texts in contemporary Christianity. Render the Greek precisely. Note the specific context (idolatry → disordered desire) without importing modern categories. Present the text; do not moralize. |
| 3:21-26 | The "righteousness of God" passage — the densest cluster of soteriological vocabulary in the NT. Every major term appears: dikaiosynē, pistis, charis, apolytrōsis, hilastērion, haima (blood). Render each term with full weight. |
| 4:1-25 | Abraham's faith. Paul's reading of Genesis 15:6 ("Abraham believed God and it was credited to him as righteousness") is the linchpin of his argument. Cross-reference TCR's Genesis 15 rendering. |
| 5:12-21 | The Adam-Christ typology. Sin entered through one man; grace overflows through one man. The grammar of 5:12 (eph' hō pantes hēmarton) is notoriously ambiguous — "because all sinned" or "in whom all sinned." Render "because all sinned" (the majority reading) and note the alternatives. |
| 6:1-14 | Baptism into Christ's death. "Shall we continue in sin that grace may abound?" Paul's most sustained treatment of baptismal theology. |
| 7:7-25 | The "I" passage. Who is the "I" who delights in the Law but cannot obey it? Options: (a) Paul's pre-conversion experience, (b) Paul's ongoing struggle as a believer, (c) a rhetorical persona representing humanity under the Law, (d) Adam. Render the Greek; present all options in notes. |
| 8:1-39 | The Spirit chapter. From "no condemnation" (8:1) to "nothing can separate us" (8:38-39). This is the theological summit of the letter. The closing peroration (8:31-39) is among the most powerful passages in the NT — render with appropriate gravity. |
| 9:6-29 | Divine election and hardening. God's freedom to choose (Jacob/Esau, Pharaoh, the potter). This passage has fueled the Calvinist/Arminian debate for centuries. Render the Greek without softening the sovereignty language. Present interpretive options in notes. |
| 9:30-10:13 | Israel's stumbling. Christ as the telos of the Law (10:4) — does telos mean "end/termination" or "goal/fulfillment"? Both are lexically valid. Render "the culmination of the Law" and note the range. |
| 11:25-36 | The mystery of Israel's salvation. "All Israel will be saved" (11:26) — who is "all Israel"? Ethnic Israel? The elect? The church? Render the Greek and present the options. The doxology (11:33-36) is a poetic climax — render with line breaks. |
| 13:1-7 | Submission to governing authorities. This passage has been used to justify compliance with unjust regimes. Render the Greek precisely. Note that Paul writes from a specific political context (pre-Neronian Rome) and that the passage must be read alongside prophetic tradition (including TCR's Daniel, Isaiah, Jeremiah) which critiques unjust powers. |
| 16:1-16 | Greetings list. Phoebe is called diakonos (deacon/minister) and prostatis (patron/leader). Junia(s) is "prominent among the apostles." Render the Greek without gender-flattening: Phoebe is a diakonos, Junia is a woman (the name is feminine). Note the debates. |

## 3. Sensitive Passages

### 3.1 Romans 1:26-27 — Same-Sex Relations

This is one of the most contested passages in contemporary biblical interpretation. The Greek refers to people who "exchanged natural relations for those contrary to nature" (para physin).

**Handling:**

- Render the Greek precisely: "exchanged natural relations for those contrary to nature" (or equivalent faithful rendering).
- Do NOT soften, paraphrase, or editorialize in the rendering.
- The translator_note must explain: (a) the immediate context is idolatry and its consequences (1:18-32), (b) "contrary to nature" (para physin) is a Stoic philosophical phrase Paul adopts, (c) the passage has been read as addressing exploitative/pederastic relationships, all same-sex relations, or the reversal of creation order — present all major readings without resolving the debate.
- This is not the rendering's fight. Translate the text. Present the scholarship. Let the reader engage.

### 3.2 Romans 9:1-29 — Divine Sovereignty and Election

Paul's language about God choosing Jacob over Esau "before they had done anything good or bad" (9:11) and hardening Pharaoh's heart (9:17-18) raises the predestination question sharply.

**Handling:**

- Render the Greek without softening. "I will have mercy on whom I have mercy" (9:15). "He has mercy on whom He wills, and He hardens whom He wills" (9:18).
- The notes must present: (a) the Calvinist/Reformed reading (individual predestination), (b) the Arminian/Wesleyan reading (corporate election, foreknowledge-based), (c) the New Perspective reading (God's right to define the covenant people), (d) the Jewish reading (God's sovereignty within covenant faithfulness).
- Note that Paul himself holds the tension — 9:19-23 is a rebuke to those who demand resolution.

### 3.3 Romans 13:1-7 — Governing Authorities

**Handling:**

- Render precisely. "Let every person be subject to the governing authorities. For there is no authority except from God, and those that exist have been instituted by God."
- The notes must present the full interpretive range: (a) this is not an absolute command to obey all governments in all circumstances, (b) Paul writes from a specific context (a relatively benign Roman administration), (c) the OT prophetic tradition (Daniel, Isaiah, Jeremiah) regularly critiques and condemns unjust rulers, (d) the passage must be read alongside Romans 12:14-21 (overcoming evil with good) and Revelation 13 (the state as beast).
- Do NOT use this passage to make political statements. Present the text and the tradition.

## 4. Term Rendering Decisions for Romans

These supplement the Default Term Register in the multi-agent consistency rules:

| Term | Default | Romans-specific guidance |
|---|---|---|
| dikaiosynē theou | the righteousness of God | LOCKED phrase. Never "God's righteousness" (possessive construction loses the ambiguity). |
| dikaioō | justify | Never "make righteous" in the rendering (that resolves the debate). "Justify" preserves the forensic/relational ambiguity. |
| hilastērion (3:25) | mercy seat | Not "propitiation" or "expiation" in the rendering. Cross-reference kapporet in Leviticus 16. |
| pistis Christou | faith in Christ | Default objective genitive. Note subjective option ("faithfulness of Christ") prominently. |
| mē genoito | By no means! | LOCKED. |
| sarx | flesh | Never "sinful nature." |
| nomos (Torah reference) | the Law | Capitalized with article when referring to Mosaic Torah. |
| telos nomou (10:4) | the culmination of the Law | Not "the end of the Law" (too abrupt) or "the goal of the Law" (too teleological). "Culmination" holds both senses. Note the debate. |
| pas Israēl (11:26) | all Israel | Do not define "all Israel" in the rendering. Let the notes carry the interpretive weight. |
| logikē latreia (12:1) | your reasonable service / your spiritual worship | Render "your true worship" — logikos means "of the logos/rational/genuine." Note that KJV's "reasonable service" and NIV's "true and proper worship" both capture something. |

## 5. Generation Plan

Romans should be generated in sections aligned with the letter's argument:

| Batch | Chapters | Description | Verses (approx.) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 1:1-17 | Introduction and thesis | 17 |
| 2 | 1:18-3:20 | Universal sin — Gentile, Jewish, all | 72 |
| 3 | 3:21-4:25 | Justification by faith, Abraham | 30 |
| 4 | 5:1-8:39 | Results of justification: peace, freedom from sin/law/death, life in the Spirit | 147 |
| 5 | 9:1-11:36 | Israel: election, stumbling, salvation | 98 |
| 6 | 12:1-15:13 | Ethical exhortation | 82 |
| 7 | 15:14-16:27 | Closing, greetings, doxology | 41 |

**Note:** Batch 4 (chs. 5-8) is the largest and could be split into 5:1-6:23 and 7:1-8:39 if needed. The argumentative arc, however, is continuous — splitting should be done only for practical reasons, and cross-batch consistency must be verified.

Each batch should be generated, QA'd, and verified before proceeding to the next.
