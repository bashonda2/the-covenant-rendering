# The Covenant Rendering — Joshua Briefing Addendum

## Purpose

Joshua is the hinge between promise and fulfillment. The Pentateuch ends with Israel on the plains of Moab, the land promised but not yet entered. Joshua tells the story of entry, conquest, and settlement — the land oath sworn to Abraham (Genesis 15:18-21) finally becoming reality.

The book operates on two levels: (1) a military-historical narrative of Israel taking possession of Canaan, and (2) a theological argument that the land is God's gift, the conquest is God's action, and Israel's possession depends entirely on covenant faithfulness. The translator notes must surface both levels. Without the theological framing, Joshua reads as ancient war propaganda. With it, Joshua is the first test of whether Israel will keep the covenant Deuteronomy demanded.

This addendum supplements (does not replace) the master generation prompt, addendum v1.2 (Theologically Rich Terms Register), and quality correction addendum v1.3 (three non-negotiable rules). All previous rules remain in full effect.

## 1. Conquest Vocabulary

Joshua introduces military and conquest-specific terms that do not appear (or appear rarely) in the Pentateuch. These must be rendered consistently and their theology explained in notes.

| Hebrew | Transliteration | Rendering | Theology | Key chapters |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| חֵרֶם | cherem | devoted to destruction / set apart for destruction | NOT ordinary warfare. Cherem is a sacred ban — everything (people, animals, goods) is "devoted" to God by being destroyed. It is the theological opposite of offering: where an offering gives the best to God, cherem gives everything to God by removing it from human use entirely. The concept is deeply troubling to modern readers — the notes must acknowledge this honestly without apologizing for or sanitizing the text. | 6, 7, 8, 10, 11 |
| יָרַשׁ | yarash | take possession / dispossess | The verb of the land promise. It means both "to possess/inherit" and "to dispossess" — Israel possesses by dispossessing. The double meaning is theologically significant: gift and displacement are inseparable in the conquest narrative. | Throughout |
| נַחֲלָה | nachalah | inheritance / allotment | The land as Israel's inherited portion from God. Not earned — received. Each tribe's nachalah is their divinely assigned territory. This term dominates chapters 13-21. | 13-21 |
| גְּבוּל | gevul | border / boundary / territory | The precise territorial demarcation of each tribe's allotment. Chapters 15-19 are dense with boundary descriptions. | 13-19 |
| עִיר מִקְלָט | ir miqlat | city of refuge | A city designated for someone who kills accidentally (manslaughter vs. murder distinction). The go'el hadam (avenger of blood) cannot touch the person within the city of refuge. This institution appears in Numbers 35 and is implemented in Joshua 20. | 20 |
| גֹּאֵל הַדָּם | go'el hadam | avenger of blood | The kinsman-redeemer (go'el) in the specific context of blood vengeance. The same go'el concept from the register, but here applied to justice for the dead. The city of refuge protects from the go'el hadam. | 20 |

**Critical note for translator_notes:** The cherem concept will be the most theologically difficult term in Joshua. The notes should:
- Define cherem accurately (sacred destruction, not mere warfare)
- Acknowledge the moral difficulty for modern readers
- Note that the text presents this as divine command, not human initiative
- Avoid both sanitizing the text and offering modern moral justification
- Let the text speak and document what it says

## 2. Key Themes for Notes

### 2.1 Promise Fulfillment

Joshua is the payoff of Genesis 12-15. Every land allotment, every border description, every conquered city is the narrative claiming: "God kept His word." The notes should make these connections:
- Joshua 1:2-6 → Genesis 15:18-21, Deuteronomy 34:4
- Joshua 21:43-45 → The explicit fulfillment summary
- Joshua 23:14 → "Not one word has failed"

### 2.2 The Law of Moses

Joshua's success is explicitly tied to obedience to "the book of the law of Moses" (torah moshe). This phrase appears at key structural moments (1:7-8, 8:31-35, 23:6). The notes should flag each occurrence and its narrative function.

### 2.3 Covenant Continuity

Joshua is Deuteronomy enacted. The blessings/curses of Deuteronomy 28 begin playing out immediately:
- Obedience → Jericho falls (ch 6)
- Disobedience → Ai defeats Israel (ch 7)
- Covenant renewal at Shechem (ch 24) echoes Sinai

### 2.4 Geography as Theology

The land descriptions (especially chapters 13-21) are not filler. They are the theological proof that God's promise was fulfilled in specific, verifiable territory. The notes should treat boundary descriptions with respect, not as tedious lists.

## 4. Watch Chapters

These chapters require close QA attention for theological density or translation difficulty:

### Chapter 1 — The Commissioning
Joshua receives the mandate. The threefold "be strong and courageous" (chazaq ve'ematz) is a literary structure. The notes should connect to Deuteronomy 31:7-8, 23 where Moses uses the same language. The torah meditation command (1:8) is programmatic for the whole book.

### Chapter 2 — Rahab
Rahab's confession (2:9-11) is a gentile's theology of the exodus — she recounts God's acts and concludes "the LORD your God is God in heaven above and on the earth below." The chesed exchange (2:12-14) is covenant language between an outsider and Israel. This chapter has New Testament resonance (Hebrews 11:31, James 2:25).

### Chapter 5 — Circumcision, Passover, and the Commander
Three events packed with theology: (1) mass circumcision at Gilgal — the wilderness generation was uncircumcised, and the covenant sign must be renewed before conquest begins; (2) Passover in the promised land — the bookend of the exodus Passover in Exodus 12; (3) the mysterious "commander of the LORD's army" (sar tseva YHWH) and the holy ground declaration (5:15), echoing Moses at the bush (Exodus 3:5).

### Chapter 6 — Jericho
The fall of Jericho is liturgical warfare, not conventional siege. Seven priests, seven trumpets, seven days, the ark leading. The notes should emphasize the ritual/processional character. Cherem is applied fully — Rahab's household exempted by the scarlet cord (connecting to 2:18-21).

### Chapter 7 — Achan
The first failure. Achan violates the cherem by taking devoted items. The corporate nature of Israelite identity is key — "Israel has sinned" (7:11), not just Achan. The punishment is severe and corporate. The notes should handle this honestly without editorializing.

### Chapter 10 — The Long Day
"Sun, stand still at Gibeon" (10:12-14). The most discussed passage in Joshua. The notes should: (1) present the Hebrew text accurately, (2) note that the language is poetic (quoted from the Book of Jashar), (3) acknowledge the interpretive debate (literal solar event, refraction/hail phenomenon, poetic hyperbole, or theophanic language) without picking one, (4) note the theological point: God fights for Israel to a degree without precedent — "there has been no day like it before or since."

### Chapters 13-21 — Land Allotment
Nine chapters of tribal boundaries and city lists. These are dense with place names and geographic terms. The notes should:
- Explain the significance of each tribe's allotment
- Note any fulfilled promises (e.g., Caleb's inheritance in 14:6-15 fulfilling Numbers 14:24)
- Flag the Levitical cities (ch 21) as the implementation of Deuteronomy 18:1-2
- NOT treat these chapters as less important than narrative chapters

### Chapter 23 — Joshua's Farewell
Joshua's first speech echoes Moses's farewell in Deuteronomy. The conditional nature of land possession is emphasized: obedience → blessing, disobedience → loss of the land. This is the Deuteronomic theology in action.

### Chapter 24 — Covenant Renewal at Shechem
The theological climax of the book. Joshua recites salvation history (24:2-13), issues the famous challenge "choose this day whom you will serve" (24:15), and mediates a covenant renewal. The berit register term should get expanded_rendering treatment here. The stone witness (24:26-27) echoes covenant witnesses throughout the Pentateuch.

## 6. Tone Guidance

Joshua is narrative with embedded speeches, lists, and one major poetic fragment (10:12-13). The renderings should:
- Read as vivid, clear narrative — Joshua is a page-turner, not a rulebook
- Handle military language directly without sanitizing or glorifying
- Render speeches with the weight of covenant authority (Joshua speaks as Moses's successor)
- Treat geographic lists with precision and respect — they are theological documentation, not filler
- Let the text's moral complexity stand without editorial resolution

Avoid:
- Notes that moralize about the conquest — document the text, don't defend or attack it
- Treating chapters 13-21 as less important than chapters 1-12
- Glossing over the cherem passages — translate them accurately and let the notes explain the concept
- Modern military language (don't say "battalion" or "campaign headquarters") — keep the ancient vocabulary

Do:
- Connect back to Pentateuch promises at every opportunity
- Explain why Rahab matters (a gentile inside Israel's story)
- Flag the structural parallel: Red Sea crossing (Exodus 14) → Jordan crossing (Joshua 3-4)
- Note when the text itself signals fulfilled promises ("as the LORD had said," "according to the word of the LORD")

*Joshua is where the promise becomes real estate. Every boundary marker, every conquered city, every tribal allotment is Genesis 15 made physical. The reader should finish Joshua knowing: God keeps His word — in dirt and stone, not just in speeches.*
