# The Covenant Rendering — Leviticus Briefing Addendum

## Purpose

Leviticus is the theological center of the Pentateuch. It sits between the exodus (God rescuing Israel) and the wilderness journey (Israel learning to live with God). Its subject is a single question: **How does a holy God dwell among an unholy people?**

The answer is the sacrificial system, the priesthood, the purity laws, and the holiness code — all of which exist to make ongoing divine presence possible. Every regulation in Leviticus serves this purpose. The translator notes must make this purpose visible, or the book reads as an arbitrary rule list.

This addendum supplements (does not replace) the master generation prompt, addendum v1.2 (Theologically Rich Terms Register), and quality correction addendum v1.3 (three non-negotiable rules). All previous rules remain in full effect.

---

## 1. Sacrificial Vocabulary — The Five Offerings

Leviticus 1-7 establishes five distinct offering types. Each has its own theology. The renderings and notes must distinguish them clearly — they are not interchangeable.

| Hebrew | Transliteration | Rendering | Theology | Key chapters |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| עֹלָה | olah | burnt offering | Total surrender — the entire animal is consumed by fire. Nothing is kept back. Represents complete devotion to God. | 1, 6:8-13 |
| מִנְחָה | minchah | grain offering | Tribute/gift — the non-animal offering. Represents the fruit of human labor offered to God. | 2, 6:14-23 |
| שְׁלָמִים | shelamim | peace offering (or fellowship offering) | Shared meal — portions go to God (fat), the priest, and the worshipper. The only offering the worshipper eats. Represents restored relationship and communion. From the root shalom. | 3, 7:11-36 |
| חַטָּאת | chata't | sin offering (or purification offering) | Purification — cleanses the sanctuary from the contamination of sin. The blood is applied to sacred objects, not primarily to the person. The offering purifies the space so God can remain present. | 4, 6:24-30 |
| אָשָׁם | asham | guilt offering (or reparation offering) | Restitution — addresses specific wrongs that require compensation. Involves repayment plus a penalty. Covers trespass against holy things or against others. | 5, 7:1-10 |

**Critical note for translator_notes:** When these terms appear, the notes should explain which offering is in view and why it matters — not just identify the Hebrew word. The reader needs to understand the *logic* of each sacrifice.

**Rendering rule:** Use the standard renderings above consistently. Do not alternate between "sin offering" and "purification offering" for chata't within the same chapter without a note explaining the choice.

## 2. Purity Vocabulary — Clean/Unclean

The Hebrew purity system is NOT about hygiene. The translator notes must make this clear whenever tamei/tahor language appears.

| Hebrew | Transliteration | Rendering | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| טָמֵא | tamei | unclean / impure | Ritually unfit for God's presence. Not morally "dirty" — a woman after childbirth (ch 12) and a man who buries the dead are not sinful, but they have contacted the boundary between life and death and need restoration before approaching God. |
| טָהוֹר | tahor | clean / pure | Ritually fit for God's presence. The normal state of an Israelite. |
| טִהֵר | tiher | cleanse / purify | The process of restoring someone from tamei to tahor status. |
| נִדָּה | niddah | impurity / menstrual impurity | A state of ritual separation. Not a moral judgment. |
| צָרַעַת | tsara'at | skin disease / leprous condition | NOT modern leprosy (Hansen's disease). A visible skin condition that renders a person tamei. The priest functions as diagnostician (chs 13-14). Render as "skin disease" or "leprous condition" with a note that this is not Hansen's disease. |

**Key principle for notes:** Purity and impurity are about proximity to God's holy presence, not about sin or hygiene. Impurity is often contracted through normal life events (birth, death, illness, bodily functions). The system provides a path *back* to God's presence — it is restorative, not punitive.

## 3. Holiness Vocabulary

| Hebrew | Transliteration | Rendering | Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| קָדוֹשׁ | qadosh | holy | Set apart. The core concept of Leviticus. Always include expanded_rendering when God's holiness is the subject (especially 11:44-45, 19:2, 20:26). |
| קָדַשׁ | qadash | consecrate / sanctify / make holy | The verb form — the act of setting apart. |
| חֹל | chol | common / ordinary | The opposite of qadosh. Not evil — just not set apart. |
| חִלֵּל | chillel | profane / desecrate | To make holy things common — to collapse the distinction between qadosh and chol. |

**The Leviticus spectrum:** Holy (qadosh) → Common (chol) → Unclean (tamei). The entire book operates on this spectrum. Movement toward holiness is the goal. Movement toward impurity threatens God's presence. The sacrificial system and purity laws manage this spectrum.

## 5. Watch Chapters

These chapters require close QA attention for theological density:

### Chapter 1-7 — The Offering System
The five offerings must be clearly distinguished in both renderings and notes. The reader should finish chapter 7 understanding why five different offerings exist and what each one accomplishes.

### Chapter 10 — Nadab and Abihu
"They offered strange fire before the LORD, which He had not commanded them." The note must connect back to Exodus 24:1-2 (these same men ate and drank on Sinai) and forward to the holiness standards that follow. The phrase "strange fire" (esh zarah) needs a key_term entry.

### Chapter 16 — Yom Kippur (Day of Atonement)
The most important chapter in Leviticus. Multiple register terms converge:
- kippur — the central verb of the chapter
- kapporet — the mercy seat / atonement cover where blood is applied
- qadosh — the holy of holies (qodesh haqqodashim)
- azazel — the scapegoat (debated term — the note should present options without picking one)
- chata't — the sin offering
All key verses should have expanded_rendering where register terms appear.

### Chapter 17 — Blood Theology
v11: "For the life of the flesh is in the blood, and I have given it to you on the altar to make atonement for your lives, for it is the blood that makes atonement by the life." This verse is the theological foundation of the entire sacrificial system. It MUST have an expanded_rendering explaining nephesh (life), dam (blood), and kippur (atonement) together.

### Chapter 19 — The Holiness Code
v2: "Be holy, for I the LORD your God am holy." This is the thesis of Leviticus. expanded_rendering required (qadosh).
v18: "You shall love your neighbor as yourself." The most quoted verse from Leviticus. key_term entry for re'akha ("your neighbor") — who counts as neighbor? The note should flag this as Jesus's most-cited OT verse.

### Chapter 23 — The Festival Calendar
Seven appointed times (mo'adim). Each festival should get a brief note explaining its place in the agricultural and theological year. Yom Kippur (23:27-28) links back to chapter 16.

### Chapter 25 — Jubilee
The go'el/ga'al register term comes alive here. Land redemption, debt release, kinsman-redeemer obligations. expanded_rendering for go'el in the land-redemption context. The Jubilee concept (yovel) needs a key_term entry.

### Chapter 26 — Blessings and Curses
Covenant consequences. The berit register term appears repeatedly (v9, v15, v25, v42, v44, v45). v42 is critical: God remembers His covenant with Jacob, Isaac, and Abraham (reverse order — the note should catch this). expanded_rendering for berit in v42-45 where covenant memory becomes the basis for future restoration.

## 6. Tone Guidance

Leviticus is detailed, repetitive, and procedural. The renderings should be clear and precise without becoming sterile. The translator notes are where the theology lives — they must consistently answer the question: **Why does this matter?**

Avoid:
- Notes that simply restate the verse in different words
- Notes that say "this regulation is part of the holiness code" without explaining what it accomplishes
- Treating purity laws as primitive or embarrassing — they are a coherent theological system

Do:
- Explain the logic behind each regulation (why this offering? why this procedure? why this distinction?)
- Connect individual laws to the overarching question: How does a holy God dwell among an unholy people?
- Use the sacrificial vocabulary table consistently
- Flag intertextual connections (especially to Exodus tabernacle instructions and forward to prophetic literature)

---

*Leviticus is where The Covenant Rendering proves its value most. Any translation can render Genesis narrative or Exodus drama. Making Leviticus comprehensible, theologically coherent, and genuinely illuminating for a modern reader — that is the test.*
